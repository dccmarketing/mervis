10,000 BC Garbage became an issue as people begin to establish permanent settlements.
7,000 BC Man learned how to remelt metals, which was the first appearance of true recycling. People discovered that metals and glass can be endlessly recycled to save a lot of energy.
2,000 BC Bronze scrap recovery systems were in place in Europe.
400 BC The first municipal dump was established in Athens, Greece.
1425 AD Before the printing press was invented, lead was used for pipes, stained-glass windows, roofing, etc. and lasted for many years or centuries before being recycled. The lead used for letters was sometimes returned to the melting pot after only a few days.
1769 The steam engine was invented and began the Industrial Revolution.
1776 The first metal recycling in America occured when patriots in New York City melt down a statue of King George III and make it into bullets.
1777 The first iron ship was built. Steam engines and iron ships provided a major stimulus for metal recycling.
1800 The demand for metals grew explosively, but as most machines at that time were built to last, it usually took decades before they became available for recycling. This lead to a shortage of scrap metal.
1810 The tin can was patented in London by Peter Durand.
1897 The first recycling center was established in New York City.
1904 Large-scale aluminum recycling began in Chicago and Cleveland.
1914 World War I began and consumption of steel accelerated quickly leading to a severe shortage of raw materials. A sharp increase in the local collection of scrap metals followed.
1917 Shortages of raw materials during World War I prompted the federal government to start the Waste Reclamation Service. Every article of waste was considered valuable for industry.
1935 The first beer can was produced. Over the next six months, sales increased 550%.
1942 Americans collected rubber, paper, glass, metals, and fats to help the war effort with recyclable materials.
1958 The automobile shredder was invented, making dismantling ships and buildings faster and cheaper.
1960 Pop tops for beverage cans were invented. Iron City Beer in Pittsburgh was the first to try the invention and sales increased immediately.
1965 The Federal Government realized that garbage had become a major problem and enacted the Solid Waste Disposal Act.
1968 The U.S. aluminum industry began recycling discarded aluminum products, from beverage cans to window blinds.
1970 United States Environmental Protection Agency was created.

Millions of people rally nationwide on April 22 to celebrate the first Earth Day.
1971 Oregon passed the nation's first bottle bill. By offering cash for aluminum, glass, and plastic containers, it prevented about 7% of garbage from ending up in landfills.
1972 The first buy-back centers for recyclables were opened in Washington State. They accepted bottles, cans, and newspapers.
1974 The first city-wide use of curbside recycling bins occured in University City, Missouri for collecting newspapers.
1976 Rhode Island enacted the nation's first statewide mandatory recycling law.

The city of San Francisco met its goal of recycling 25% of commercial and residential waste.
1986 Large-scale aluminum recycling began in Chicago and Cleveland.
1987 The Mobro, a Long Island garbage barge, was turned away by six states and three countries. This incident sparked public awareness of the growing garbage disposal problem.
1989 EPA issued “An Agenda for Action,” with waste prevention and recycling as its first two priorities.
1990 140 recycling laws are enacted in 38 states and the District of Columbia.

Researchers worked to reduce, reuse, and recycle waste from the Skylab Space Station to avoid the $50,000 per pound cost of bringing it back to Earth and the problem of creating free-floating waste in space.
1997 Americans recycled and composted 22.4% of municipal waste, compared to a 6.4% recycling rate in 1960.
2000 Cities in California were required to recycle 50% of their waste.

The “pay-as-you-throw” idea began in about 5,000 U.S. cities, which required citizens to pay for garbage disposal.
2007 Americans recycled and composted 33.4% of municipal waste, an increase of 11% in just 10 years.
2010 Americans recycled and composted 34.1% of municipal waste. Only 54.2% of waste ended up in a landfill. The remaining 11.7% of waste was burned and used to generate energy.
